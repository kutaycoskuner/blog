5.8. Theorem: The Factor Theorem for Polynomials

Theorem 5.8 (Satz 5.8). Let fff be a polynomial (Polynom) with deg(f)≥1\text{deg}(f) \ge 1deg(f)≥1 and assume f(z0)=0f(z_0) = 0f(z0​)=0 for some element z0z_0z0​ in the field KKK. Then, there exists a polynomial (Polynom) ggg such that deg(g)=deg(f)−1\text{deg}(g) = \text{deg}(f) - 1deg(g)=deg(f)−1 and f(z)=(z−z0)g(z) for all z∈K.f(z) = (z - z_0)g(z) \text{ for all } z \in K.f(z)=(z−z0​)g(z) for all z∈K.

Concept/Reasoning: This theorem is fundamental, as it establishes a direct link between the roots (Nullstellen) of a polynomial and its linear factors. Specifically, if z0z_0z0​ is a root of fff, then the linear expression (z−z0)(z - z_0)(z−z0​) must divide fff exactly.

Proof outline: The proof begins by demonstrating the polynomial division with remainder (Division Algorithm):

Step 1: Division with Remainder (Division mit Rest) From the Polynomial Division Algorithm (Satz 5.7), we know that for any z0∈Kz_0 \in Kz0​∈K and any polynomial fff with deg(f)≥1\text{deg}(f) \ge 1deg(f)≥1, we can divide f(z)f(z)f(z) by the linear factor (z−z0)(z - z_0)(z−z0​). This division yields a unique quotient polynomial g(z)g(z)g(z) and a remainder r∈Kr \in Kr∈K (since the divisor (z−z0)(z - z_0)(z−z0​) has degree 1, the remainder must have degree less than 1, meaning it is a constant):

f(z)=(z−z0)g(z)+r for all z∈K.f(z) = (z - z_0)g(z) + r \text{ for all } z \in K.f(z)=(z−z0​)g(z)+r for all z∈K.

Since ggg is the result of dividing a polynomial of degree deg(f)\text{deg}(f)deg(f) by a polynomial of degree 1, the degree of ggg must be deg(f)−1\text{deg}(f) - 1deg(f)−1.

deg(g)=deg(f)−1.\text{deg}(g) = \text{deg}(f) - 1.deg(g)=deg(f)−1.

Step 2: Determining the Remainder (Rest bestimmen) We evaluate the equation from Step 1 at the specific point z=z0z = z_0z=z0​:

f(z0)=(z0−z0)g(z0)+rf(z_0) = (z_0 - z_0)g(z_0) + rf(z0​)=(z0​−z0​)g(z0​)+r

Step 3: Using the Root Condition (Nullstellenbedingung nutzen) By the premise (Voraussetzung) of the theorem, z0z_0z0​ is a root, meaning f(z0)=0f(z_0) = 0f(z0​)=0. Substituting this into the result from Step 2:

0=(0)g(z0)+r0 = (0)g(z_0) + r0=(0)g(z0​)+r 0=0+r0 = 0 + r0=0+r r=0r = 0r=0

Conclusion (Schlussfolgerung) Since the remainder rrr is zero, substituting r=0r=0r=0 back into the division formula from Step 1 completes the proof:

f(z)=(z−z0)g(z).f(z) = (z - z_0)g(z).f(z)=(z−z0​)g(z).

This confirms that if z0z_0z0​ is a root, (z−z0)(z-z_0)(z−z0​) is a factor.

5.9. Corollary: Roots and Uniqueness of Polynomials

Corollary 5.9 (Korollar 5.9). Let fff and ggg be polynomials (Polynome) over the field KKK (R\mathbb{R}R or C\mathbb{C}C).

a) Number of Roots (Anzahl der Nullstellen): If a polynomial fff has a degree deg(f)=n≥0\text{deg}(f) = n \ge 0deg(f)=n≥0, then the set of its roots (Nullstellen) has at most nnn elements. That is:

deg(f)=n≥0  ⟹  ∣{z∈K:f(z)=0}∣≤n\text{deg}(f) = n \ge 0 \implies |\{z \in K : f(z) = 0\}| \le ndeg(f)=n≥0⟹∣{z∈K:f(z)=0}∣≤n

b) Uniqueness of Coefficients (Eindeutigkeit der Koeffizienten): Let f(z)f(z)f(z) and g(z)g(z)g(z) be two polynomials represented in power series form up to degree nnn:

f(z)=∑k=0nakzkandg(z)=∑k=0nbkzkf(z) = \sum_{k=0}^{n} a_k z^k \quad \text{and} \quad g(z) = \sum_{k=0}^{n} b_k z^kf(z)=k=0∑n​ak​zkandg(z)=k=0∑n​bk​zk

If the set of points where the functions are equal, {z:f(z)=g(z)}\{z : f(z) = g(z)\}{z:f(z)=g(z)}, has more than nnn elements, then their corresponding coefficients must be identical:

a0=b0,a1=b1,…,an=bna_0 = b_0, \quad a_1 = b_1, \quad \dots, \quad a_n = b_na0​=b0​,a1​=b1​,…,an​=bn​

This means that for polynomials, the coefficients (Polynomkoeffizienten) are uniquely determined (eindeutig) by the function’s values.

Furthermore (Folgerung): A key consequence of coefficient uniqueness is that the degree of the product of two non-zero polynomials is the sum of their degrees:

deg(f⋅g)=deg(f)+deg(g)\text{deg}(f \cdot g) = \text{deg}(f) + \text{deg}(g)deg(f⋅g)=deg(f)+deg(g)

Concept/Reasoning: Part (a) directly follows from the Factor Theorem (Satz 5.8). Each time a root z0z_0z0​ is found, the polynomial can be factored by (z−z0)(z - z_0)(z−z0​), reducing its degree by one. Since a polynomial of degree nnn can be factored at most nnn times, it can have at most nnn distinct roots.

Part (b) is derived by considering the difference polynomial h(z)=f(z)−g(z)h(z) = f(z) - g(z)h(z)=f(z)−g(z). If f(z)f(z)f(z) and g(z)g(z)g(z) agree at more than nnn points, h(z)h(z)h(z) has more than nnn roots. Since deg(h)≤n\text{deg}(h) \le ndeg(h)≤n, part (a) implies this is only possible if h(z)h(z)h(z) is the zero polynomial, meaning all its coefficients (which are ak−bka_k - b_kak​−bk​) are zero, thus ak=bka_k = b_kak​=bk​.

Proof Outline

Proof of Part a) (Number of Roots): We demonstrate this part using induction (Induktion) on the degree (Grad) of the polynomial fff, n=deg(f)≥0n = \text{deg}(f) \ge 0n=deg(f)≥0.

Base Case (Induktionsanfang): Let deg(f)=0\text{deg}(f) = 0deg(f)=0. This means f(z)=a0f(z) = a_0f(z)=a0​ where a0≠0a_0 \ne 0a0​=0 (otherwise deg(f)=−∞\text{deg}(f) = -\inftydeg(f)=−∞).
