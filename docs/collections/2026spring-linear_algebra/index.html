Introduction

Linear algebra is a branch of mathematics that studies vectors, vector spaces, and linear transformations between them. It provides a framework for representing and solving systems of linear equations and for describing geometric objects and transformations in a precise mathematical way. The fundamental objects in linear algebra include vectors, matrices, and scalars, which are used to model relationships between quantities.

One of the central ideas of linear algebra is that complex problems can often be expressed as combinations of simpler linear relationships. By organizing data into vectors and matrices, many problems can be solved efficiently using systematic algebraic methods. Concepts such as matrix multiplication, determinants, eigenvalues, and vector spaces form the core tools of this field.

Linear algebra plays a crucial role in many areas of science, engineering, and technology. In computer graphics, it is used to represent and manipulate 2D and 3D objects through transformations such as rotation, scaling, and translation. In machine learning and data science, matrices and vectors are used to represent datasets and perform operations such as regression, dimensionality reduction, and neural network computations. Physics uses linear algebra to describe systems of equations, quantum states, and transformations of physical quantities. It is also fundamental in fields such as signal processing, control systems, economics, and statistics.

Because of its wide applicability, linear algebra is considered one of the most important mathematical foundations for modern computational and scientific work.

Definition: Fields

In linear algebra, vectors are defined over a field. A field is a set of elements together with two operations, usually called addition and multiplication, that satisfy certain rules. These rules ensure that the operations behave similarly to the familiar arithmetic of real numbers.

Typical examples of fields include the real numbers R\mathbb{R}R, the rational numbers Q\mathbb{Q}Q, and the complex numbers C\mathbb{C}C.

  • Additive inverse does not exist:

0=0′0 = 0'0=0′

Definition: Subspace

Untervektorraum

Let VVV be a vector space over a field K\mathbb{K}K and let U⊆VU \subseteq VU⊆V.

The subset UUU is called a subspace (Untervektorraum, UVR) of VVV if UUU itself forms a vector space using the same operations as VVV, restricted to UUU.

The operations in VVV are

+:V×V→V+ : V \times V \to V+:V×V→V ⋅:K×V→V\cdot : \mathbb{K} \times V \to V⋅:K×V→V

For UUU to be a subspace, the restricted operations must satisfy

+:U×U→U+ : U \times U \to U+:U×U→U ⋅:K×U→U\cdot : \mathbb{K} \times U \to U⋅:K×U→U

Thus UUU must itself be a vector space over K\mathbb{K}K with these restricted operations.

Characterization of a subspace

Let VVV be a vector space over a field K\mathbb{K}K and let U⊆VU \subseteq VU⊆V.

Then UUU is a subspace of VVV if and only if the following conditions hold:

  1. The zero vector is contained in UUU:
0V∈U0_V \in U0V​∈U
  1. UUU is closed under vector addition:
u,w∈U  ⇒  u+w∈Uu, w \in U \;\Rightarrow\; u + w \in Uu,w∈U⇒u+w∈U
  1. UUU is closed under scalar multiplication:
u∈U, λ∈K  ⇒  λu∈Uu \in U,\ \lambda \in \mathbb{K} \;\Rightarrow\; \lambda u \in Uu∈U, λ∈K⇒λu∈U

If these three properties are satisfied, then UUU forms a vector space over K\mathbb{K}K with the operations inherited from VVV.

Remark

To verify that a subset U⊆VU \subseteq VU⊆V is a subspace, it is sufficient to check the three properties from the characterization above.

In particular, if U⊆VU \subseteq VU⊆V satisfies

  • U≠∅U \neq \emptysetU=∅

Definition: Generating set

Erzeugendes System

Let VVV be a vector space over a field K\mathbb{K}K and let M⊆VM \subseteq VM⊆V.

We call MMM a generating set (erzeugendes System) of VVV if

span⁡(M)=V.\operatorname{span}(M) = V.span(M)=V.

This means every vector in VVV can be written as a linear combination of vectors in MMM.

If, in addition, no proper subset of MMM is still a generating set of VVV, then MMM is called a minimal generating system (minimales erzeugendes System) of VVV.
