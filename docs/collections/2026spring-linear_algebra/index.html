Introduction

Linear algebra is a branch of mathematics that studies vectors, vector spaces, and linear transformations between them. It provides a framework for representing and solving systems of linear equations and for describing geometric objects and transformations in a precise mathematical way. The fundamental objects in linear algebra include vectors, matrices, and scalars, which are used to model relationships between quantities.

One of the central ideas of linear algebra is that complex problems can often be expressed as combinations of simpler linear relationships. By organizing data into vectors and matrices, many problems can be solved efficiently using systematic algebraic methods. Concepts such as matrix multiplication, determinants, eigenvalues, and vector spaces form the core tools of this field.

Linear algebra plays a crucial role in many areas of science, engineering, and technology. In computer graphics, it is used to represent and manipulate 2D and 3D objects through transformations such as rotation, scaling, and translation. In machine learning and data science, matrices and vectors are used to represent datasets and perform operations such as regression, dimensionality reduction, and neural network computations. Physics uses linear algebra to describe systems of equations, quantum states, and transformations of physical quantities. It is also fundamental in fields such as signal processing, control systems, economics, and statistics.

Because of its wide applicability, linear algebra is considered one of the most important mathematical foundations for modern computational and scientific work.

1. Fields

In linear algebra, vectors are defined over a field. A field is a set of elements together with two operations, usually called addition and multiplication, that satisfy certain rules. These rules ensure that the operations behave similarly to the familiar arithmetic of real numbers.

Typical examples of fields include the real numbers R\mathbb{R}R, the rational numbers Q\mathbb{Q}Q, and the complex numbers C\mathbb{C}C.

Definition: A field FFF is a set equipped with two operations:

  • Addition: +++
  • Multiplication: ⋅\cdot⋅

such that for all a,b,c∈Fa,b,c \in Fa,b,c∈F, the following axioms hold.

Examples of Fields

Natural Numbers N\mathbb{N}N (Not a Field)

The set of natural numbers N={0,1,2,3,… }\mathbb{N} = \{0,1,2,3,\dots\}N={0,1,2,3,…} is not a field.

Some field axioms are not satisfied:

  • Additive inverse does not exist:
    For example, the additive inverse of 333 would be −3-3−3, but −3∉N-3 \notin \mathbb{N}−3∈/N.

  • Multiplicative inverse does not exist:
    For example, the multiplicative inverse of 222 would be 12\frac{1}{2}21​, but 12∉N\frac{1}{2} \notin \mathbb{N}21​∈/N.

Therefore N\mathbb{N}N does not satisfy the field axioms.

Rational Numbers Q\mathbb{Q}Q

The set of rational numbers

Q={pq∣p,q∈Z, q≠0}\mathbb{Q} = \left\{\frac{p}{q} \mid p,q \in \mathbb{Z},\ q \ne 0 \right\}Q={qp​∣p,q∈Z, q=0}

forms a field.

  • Addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division (except by zero) are all defined.
  • Every non-zero element has a multiplicative inverse.

Example:

35−1=53\frac{3}{5}^{-1} = \frac{5}{3}53​−1=35​

Real Numbers R\mathbb{R}R

The real numbers R\mathbb{R}R also form a field.

They include:

  • rational numbers
  • irrational numbers such as 2\sqrt{2}2​ and π\piπ

All field axioms are satisfied.

The complex numbers

C={a+bi∣a,b∈R, i2=−1}\mathbb{C} = \{a + bi \mid a,b \in \mathbb{R},\ i^2 = -1\}C={a+bi∣a,b∈R, i2=−1}

2. Vector Spaces

A vector space (or linear space) is a set of elements called vectors together with two operations:

  • vector addition
  • scalar multiplication

The scalars come from a field FFF (for example R\mathbb{R}R or C\mathbb{C}C).

A vector space is denoted by VVV and its elements are vectors v,w,u∈Vv,w,u \in Vv,w,u∈V.

Definition: Let FFF be a field. A vector space over FFF is a set VVV together with

  • a vector addition +:V×V→V+: V \times V \to V+:V×V→V
  • a scalar multiplication ⋅:F×V→V\cdot : F \times V \to V⋅:F×V→V

such that for all u,v,w∈Vu,v,w \in Vu,v,w∈V and all a,b∈Fa,b \in Fa,b∈F the following axioms hold.

Remarks

  1. The symbols “+” and “·” do not always refer to the same operation in different contexts. Their meaning depends on the structure being considered.

A field is denoted by K\mathbb{K}K (Körper). Operations in a field act on elements of K\mathbb{K}K:

x+Ky∈K,x⋅Ky∈Kx +_{\mathbb{K}} y \in \mathbb{K}, \quad x \cdot_{\mathbb{K}} y \in \mathbb{K}x+K​y∈K,x⋅K​y∈K

Both operations are internal to K\mathbb{K}K.

A vector space VVV is defined over a field K\mathbb{K}K. There are two different operations:

Vector addition:

u+Vv∈Vu +_{V} v \in Vu+V​v∈V

Scalar multiplication:

a⋅K,Vv∈Vwith a∈K,  v∈Va \cdot_{\mathbb{K},V} v \in V \quad \text{with } a \in \mathbb{K},\; v \in Va⋅K,V​v∈Vwith a∈K,v∈V
  • The element 000 is called the zero vector.
  • The element −v-v−v is called the additive inverse of vvv.

x+Ky,x⋅Ky∈Kx +_{\mathbb{K}} y,\quad x \cdot_{\mathbb{K}} y \in \mathbb{K}x+K​y,x⋅K​y∈K

Conclusion

The same symbols “+” and “·” represent different operations depending on whether we are in:
