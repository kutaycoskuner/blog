2. Proof and Proof Principles (Beweismethoden)

A proof is a logical sequence of statements that establishes the truth of a theorem, starting from axioms and previously proven statements. The following are the most common methodologies used to structure a proof.

2.1. Direct Proof (Direkter Beweis)

A direct proof starts by assuming the premise (ppp) is true, and then uses a chain of logical deductions, definitions, and known theorems to show that the conclusion (qqq) must also be true. This is the most straightforward method.

Strategy to Prove p→qp \to qp→q: Assume ppp. Show p→⋯→qp \to \dots \to qp→⋯→q.

Example: Prove that if nnn is an odd integer, then n2n^2n2 is an odd integer.

  1. Assume ppp (Premise): Let nnn be an odd integer.
  2. Definition: By definition, an odd integer can be written as n=2k+1n = 2k + 1n=2k+1 for some integer kkk.
  3. Deduction: Calculate n2n^2n2: n2=(2k+1)2=4k2+4k+1n^2 = (2k + 1)^2 = 4k^2 + 4k + 1n2=(2k+1)2=4k2+4k+1.
  4. Conclusion (qqq): Factor n2=2(2k2+2k)+1n^2 = 2(2k^2 + 2k) + 1n2=2(2k2+2k)+1. Since 2k2+2k2k^2 + 2k2k2+2k is an integer, n2n^2n2 is in the form 2m+12m + 12m+1, meaning n2n^2n2 is an odd integer.

2.2. Proof by Contraposition (Indirekter Beweis - Beweis durch Kontraposition)

This method relies on the fact that an implication p→qp \to qp→q is logically equivalent to its contrapositive ¬q→¬p\neg q \to \neg p¬q→¬p. Instead of proving the original statement, you prove the contrapositive, which often simplifies the algebra.

Strategy to Prove p→qp \to qp→q: Prove ¬q→¬p\neg q \to \neg p¬q→¬p. (Assume ¬q\neg q¬q. Show ¬q→⋯→¬p\neg q \to \dots \to \neg p¬q→⋯→¬p.)

Example: Prove that if n2n^2n2 is an even integer, then nnn is an even integer.

  1. Original Statement (p→qp \to qp→q): n2n^2n2 is even →\to→ nnn is even.
  2. Contrapositive (¬q→¬p\neg q \to \neg p¬q→¬p): Assume the negation of the conclusion: nnn is odd.
  3. Deduction: If nnn is odd, then n=2k+1n=2k+1n=2k+1. As shown in the direct proof example, n2=2(2k2+2k)+1n^2 = 2(2k^2 + 2k) + 1n2=2(2k2+2k)+1, which is odd.
  4. Conclusion (¬p\neg p¬p): n2n^2n2 is odd, which is the negation of the original premise (n2n^2n2 is even). Since the contrapositive is true, the original statement is true.

2.3. Proof by Contradiction (Indirekter Beweis - Beweis durch Widerspruch)

This method involves assuming the statement you want to prove (ppp) is false (i.e., you assume ¬p\neg p¬p is true). You then proceed with logical steps until you reach a conclusion that is known to be false or creates a logical impossibility (a contradiction, r∧¬rr \land \neg rr∧¬r). Reaching a contradiction means your initial assumption (¬p\neg p¬p) was wrong, which proves the original statement (ppp) must be true.

Strategy to Prove ppp: Assume ¬p\neg p¬p. Show ¬p→⋯→(r∧¬r)\neg p \to \dots \to (r \land \neg r)¬p→⋯→(r∧¬r).

Example: Prove that 2\sqrt{2}2​ is irrational.

  1. Statement (ppp): 2\sqrt{2}2​ is irrational.
  2. Assumption for Contradiction (¬p\neg p¬p): Assume 2\sqrt{2}2​ is rational. By definition, 2=ab\sqrt{2} = \frac{a}{b}2​=ba​, where aaa and bbb are integers with no common factors (simplified fraction).
  3. Deduction: Squaring both sides gives 2=a2b22 = \frac{a^2}{b^2}2=b2a2​, or a2=2b2a^2 = 2b^2a2=2b2. This means a2a^2a2 is even, so aaa must also be even (by contraposition). Let a=2ka = 2ka=2k.
  4. Deduction: Substituting a=2ka=2ka=2k back: (2k)2=2b2(2k)^2 = 2b^2(2k)2=2b2, so 4k2=2b24k^2 = 2b^24k2=2b2, which simplifies to 2k2=b22k^2 = b^22k2=b2. This means b2b^2b2 is even, so bbb must also be even.
  5. Contradiction: We concluded that both aaa and bbb are even, meaning they share a common factor of 2. This contradicts our initial assumption (Step 2) that aaa and bbb had no common factors.
  6. Conclusion: The initial assumption (¬p\neg p¬p) must be false, therefore 2\sqrt{2}2​ is irrational (ppp).

2.4. Proof of Equivalence through Two Implications (Beweis der Äquivalenz durch zwei Implikationen)

To prove that two statements ppp and qqq are logically equivalent (p↔qp \leftrightarrow qp↔q, read as ”ppp if and only if qqq”), we must prove that each statement implies the other. This decomposes the proof into two separate, simpler direct proofs.

Strategy to Prove p↔qp \leftrightarrow qp↔q: Prove (p→q)(p \to q)(p→q) AND (q→p)(q \to p)(q→p).

Example: Prove that An integer nnn is even if and only if n−1n-1n−1 is odd.

  1. Goal: Prove (n is even→n−1 is odd)(n \text{ is even} \to n-1 \text{ is odd})(n is even→n−1 is odd) and (n−1 is odd→n is even)(n-1 \text{ is odd} \to n \text{ is even})(n−1 is odd→n is even).
  2. Part 1 (p→qp \to qp→q): Assume nnn is even. By definition, n=2kn = 2kn=2k for some integer kkk.
  3. Deduction: Then n−1=2k−1n-1 = 2k - 1n−1=2k−1. Since 2k2k2k is even, 2k−12k-12k−1 is odd.
  4. Part 2 (q→pq \to pq→p): Assume n−1n-1n−1 is odd. By definition, n−1=2m+1n-1 = 2m + 1n−1=2m+1 for some integer mmm.
  5. Deduction: Then n=(2m+1)+1=2m+2n = (2m + 1) + 1 = 2m + 2n=(2m+1)+1=2m+2. Factoring gives n=2(m+1)n = 2(m+1)n=2(m+1).
  6. Conclusion: Since m+1m+1m+1 is an integer, nnn is even.
  7. Final Conclusion: Since both implications hold, the equivalence is proven.

2.5. Proof by Case Separation (Fallunterscheidung)

This principle is used when the domain or premise (ppp) naturally splits into a finite number of distinct, exhaustive cases (p1,p2,…,pnp_1, p_2, \ldots, p_np1​,p2​,…,pn​). If you can prove the conclusion (qqq) holds for every single possible case, then the implication is proven for the entire premise.

Strategy to Prove p→qp \to qp→q: Prove (p1→q)∧(p2→q)∧…∧(pn→q)(p_1 \to q) \land (p_2 \to q) \land \ldots \land (p_n \to q)(p1​→q)∧(p2​→q)∧…∧(pn​→q).

Example: Prove that for any integer nnn, the expression n2+nn^2 + nn2+n is even.

  1. Statement (ppp): nnn is any integer.
  2. Case Separation: We separate all integers into two exhaustive cases: nnn is even (p1p_1p1​) or nnn is odd (p2p_2p2​).
  3. Case 1 (n is Even): Assume n=2kn = 2kn=2k. Deduction: n2+n=(2k)2+2k=4k2+2k=2(2k2+k)n^2 + n = (2k)^2 + 2k = 4k^2 + 2k = 2(2k^2 + k)n2+n=(2k)2+2k=4k2+2k=2(2k2+k). The result is 222 times an integer, so n2+nn^2 + nn2+n is even.
  4. Case 2 (n is Odd): Assume n=2k+1n = 2k + 1n=2k+1. Deduction: n2+n=(2k+1)2+(2k+1)=(4k2+4k+1)+(2k+1)=4k2+6k+2=2(2k2+3k+1)n^2 + n = (2k+1)^2 + (2k+1) = (4k^2 + 4k + 1) + (2k + 1) = 4k^2 + 6k + 2 = 2(2k^2 + 3k + 1)n2+n=(2k+1)2+(2k+1)=(4k2+4k+1)+(2k+1)=4k2+6k+2=2(2k2+3k+1). The result is 222 times an integer, so n2+nn^2 + nn2+n is even.
  5. Final Conclusion: Since n2+nn^2 + nn2+n is even in every possible case, the statement is proven for all integers nnn.

4. Predicate Logic (Die Sprache der Prädikatenlogik)

Predicate Logic is an extension of Propositional Logic (Aussagenlogik) that overcomes certain limitations, allowing us to analyze statements involving variables, general assertions, and existence claims.

4.1. Limitations of Propositional Logic

Propositional Logic fails to adequately model complex sentences for two main reasons:

  1. Missing Rules for Negation: It provides no formal rules to determine the negation of general statements like “Every integer nnn has the property that if n2n^2n2 is even, then nnn is even.”
  2. Statements vs. Predicates: A sentence like ”n2n^2n2 is even” is not a true statement (Aussage) because its truth value depends on the variable nnn. It only becomes a statement when nnn is replaced by a concrete number.

4.2. New Elements in Predicate Logic

Predicate Logic introduces two main elements:

  1. Variables and Predicates (Prädikaten):

    • Variables are placeholders, usually x,y,z,…x, y, z, \dotsx,y,z,… or n,m,…n, m, \dotsn,m,….
    • Predicates are abstract statements that serve as “statement templates” (Aussagenschablonen). They become concrete statements (Aussagen) only after their variables are assigned a value (Belegung).
    • Example: If Even(n)\text{Even}(n)Even(n) is the predicate “The number nnn is even” (Die Zahl nnn ist gerade), then Even(5)\text{Even}(5)Even(5) is a false statement, while Even(6)\text{Even}(6)Even(6) is a true statement.

  2. Quantifiers (Quantoren or Quantifikatoren):
    Quantifiers are used to model the restrictions or choices of variables (“There exists a number such that…”, “For all numbers holds…”). They allow us to turn a predicate into a statement (quantification / Quantifizierung).

    • Universal Quantifier (∀\forall∀ / Allquantor): Demands the validity of the predicate for all possible assignments of a variable. Read as “for all” or “for every.”
    • Existential Quantifier (∃\exists∃ / Existenzquantor): Demands the validity of the predicate for at least one assignment of a variable. Read as “there exists.”
    • Universe: Quantification implicitly assumes a universe or domain (Grundbereich) for the variables (e.g., Z,N,R\mathbb{Z}, \mathbb{N}, \mathbb{R}Z,N,R).

4.3. Formalization of Statements

Statements that contain phrases like “for every” or “there exists” can be formally written using quantifiers.

Example 1: Formalize the statement: “For every integer nnn, if n2n^2n2 is even, then nnn is even.”

Using the domain of integers Z\mathbb{Z}Z implicitly: ∀n(EvenSquare(n)⇒Even(n))\forall n (\text{EvenSquare}(n) \Rightarrow \text{Even}(n))∀n(EvenSquare(n)⇒Even(n)).
Specifying the domain explicitly: ∀n∈Z(EvenSquare(n)⇒Even(n))\forall n \in \mathbb{Z} (\text{EvenSquare}(n) \Rightarrow \text{Even}(n))∀n∈Z(EvenSquare(n)⇒Even(n)).

Example 2: Formalize the statement: “There is no rational number xxx such that x2=2x^2 = 2x2=2.”

This is formalized as the negation of an existence claim: ¬∃x(x∈Q∧x2=2)\neg \exists x (x \in \mathbb{Q} \land x^2 = 2)¬∃x(x∈Q∧x2=2), where Q\mathbb{Q}Q is the set of rational numbers. A comma between predicates often implies the logical AND (∧\land∧): ¬∃x∈Q,x2=2\neg \exists x \in \mathbb{Q}, x^2 = 2¬∃x∈Q,x2=2.

4.4. De Morgan’s Laws for Quantifiers

Quantifiers behave according to rules similar to De Morgan’s laws for logical operators:

  1. The negation of a universal statement (¬∀xF\neg \forall x F¬∀xF) is equivalent to an existential statement with the negated predicate (∃x¬F\exists x \neg F∃x¬F).
  2. The negation of an existential statement (¬∃xF\neg \exists x F¬∃xF) is equivalent to a universal statement with the negated predicate (∀x¬F\forall x \neg F∀x¬F).

Example: The statement ¬∃x∈Q,x2=2\neg \exists x \in \mathbb{Q}, x^2 = 2¬∃x∈Q,x2=2 is equivalent to ∀x∈Q,x2≠2\forall x \in \mathbb{Q}, x^2 \neq 2∀x∈Q,x2=2.

5. Set Theory (Mengenlehre)

Set Theory is the mathematical study of collections of objects. The language of sets is used universally across mathematics, providing a formal way to define and operate on groups of objects.

5.1. Basic Definitions (Grundlegende Definitionen)

Set and Element (Menge und Element)

Definition: A set (Menge) is an unordered collection of distinct objects. These objects are called elements (Elemente) or members.

We use the notation x∈Ax \in Ax∈A to say that xxx is an element of set AAA (”xxx ist ein Element von AAA”).
We use x∉Ax \notin Ax∈/A to denote that xxx is not an element of AAA.

Set Representation (Darstellung von Mengen)

Sets can be defined in two common ways:

  1. Listing elements (Aufzählung der Elemente): A={1,3,5,7}A = \{1, 3, 5, 7\}A={1,3,5,7}. The order does not matter.
  2. Set-Builder Notation (Prädikat-Notation): Defining the set based on a property (Eigenschaft) or predicate (Prädikat) P(x):A={x∣P(x)}P(x) : A = \{x \mid P(x)\}P(x):A={x∣P(x)}

    Example: The set of all even integers is E={x∈Z∣∃k∈Z:x=2k}E = \{x \in \mathbb{Z} \mid \exists k \in \mathbb{Z}: x = 2k\}E={x∈Z∣∃k∈Z:x=2k}.

Cardinality (Mächtigkeit)

The cardinality (Mächtigkeit) of a set AAA, denoted ∣A∣|A|∣A∣, is the number of distinct elements in the set.

  • A set is finite (endlich) if its cardinality is a natural number; otherwise, it is infinite (unendlich), e.g., ∣Z∣=∞|\mathbb{Z}| = \infty∣Z∣=∞.

Subsets and Set Equality (Teilmengen und Mengenidentität)

Subset (Teilmenge): AAA is a subset of BBB, written A⊆BA \subseteq BA⊆B, if every element of AAA is also an element of BBB. Formally: ∀x(x∈A⇒x∈B)\forall x (x \in A \Rightarrow x \in B)∀x(x∈A⇒x∈B).

Proper Subset (Echte Teilmenge): AAA is a proper subset of BBB, written A⊂BA \subset BA⊂B, if A⊆BA \subseteq BA⊆B and A≠BA \neq BA=B.

Set Equality (Mengenidentität): Two sets AAA and BBB are equal (A=BA = BA=B) if they have exactly the same elements. This is proven by showing equivalence (Äquivalenz):
A=B  ⟺  (A⊆B∧B⊆A)A = B \iff (A \subseteq B \land B \subseteq A)A=B⟺(A⊆B∧B⊆A)

Fundamental Sets

Empty Set (Leere Menge): The set with no elements, denoted ∅\emptyset∅ or {}\{\}{}.

Universal Set (Universelle Menge): The set containing all objects currently under consideration (also called the Universe or Grundmenge), usually denoted UUU.

5.2. Set Operations (Mengenoperationen)

1. Union (Vereinigung):
The union of AAA and BBB, A∪BA \cup BA∪B, is the set containing all elements that are in AAA, or in BBB, or in both.

A∪B={x∣x∈A∨x∈B}A \cup B = \{x \mid x \in A \lor x \in B\}A∪B={x∣x∈A∨x∈B}

2. Intersection (Schnittmenge):
The intersection of AAA and BBB, A∩BA \cap BA∩B, is the set containing all elements that are in both AAA and BBB.

A∩B={x∣x∈A∧x∈B}A \cap B = \{x \mid x \in A \land x \in B\}A∩B={x∣x∈A∧x∈B}

If A∩B=∅A \cap B = \emptysetA∩B=∅, the sets are disjoint (disjunkt).

3. Set Difference (Mengendifferenz):
The difference of AAA and BBB, A∖BA \setminus BA∖B, is the set containing elements in AAA but not in BBB.
A∖B={x∣x∈A∧x∉B}A \setminus B = \{x \mid x \in A \land x \notin B\}A∖B={x∣x∈A∧x∈/B}

4. Complement (Komplement):
The complement of a set AAA, denoted Aˉ\bar{A}Aˉ or AcA^cAc (relative to the universal set UUU), is the set of all elements in UUU that are not in AAA.
Aˉ=U∖A\bar{A} = U \setminus AAˉ=U∖A

5. Power Set (Potenzmenge):
The power set (Potenzmenge) of a set AAA, P(A)\mathcal{P}(A)P(A), is the set of all possible subsets of AAA.

Example: If A={1,2}A = \{1, 2\}A={1,2}, then P(A)={∅,{1},{2},{1,2}}\mathcal{P}(A) = \{\emptyset, \{1\}, \{2\}, \{1, 2\}\}P(A)={∅,{1},{2},{1,2}}.
Cardinality: If ∣A∣=n|A| = n∣A∣=n, then ∣P(A)∣=2n|\mathcal{P}(A)| = 2^n∣P(A)∣=2n.

6. Cartesian Product (Kartesisches Produkt):
The Cartesian product (Kartesisches Produkt) of AAA and BBB, A×BA \times BA×B, is the set of all possible ordered pairs (geordnete Paare) (a,b)(a, b)(a,b) where a∈Aa \in Aa∈A and b∈Bb \in Bb∈B.
A×B={(a,b)∣a∈A∧b∈B}A \times B = \{(a, b) \mid a \in A \land b \in B\}A×B={(a,b)∣a∈A∧b∈B}

3. Elementary Number Theory (Arithmetik)

This section reviews fundamental concepts of arithmetic (Arithmetik) and number theory, which are often used to illustrate proof techniques (Beweistechniken). We begin by formalizing the notation for number sets and defining core principles like divisibility and the calculation of the Greatest Common Divisor (ggT\text{ggT}ggT).

3.1. Divisibility (Teilbarkeit)

Definition: Let aaa and bbb be integers (a,b∈Za, b \in \mathbb{Z}a,b∈Z). We say that aaa divides bbb (aaa teilt bbb), or aaa is a divisor (aaa ist ein Teiler) of bbb, written a∣ba|ba∣b, if there exists an integer k∈Zk \in \mathbb{Z}k∈Z such that ak=bak = bak=b.

Properties (Eigenschaften): Let d,a,b∈Zd, a, b \in \mathbb{Z}d,a,b∈Z.

  • If ddd divides aaa and ddd divides bbb, then ddd divides a+ba+ba+b.
  • If ddd divides aaa, then ddd divides the product ababab.

3.2. Greatest Common Divisor (Größter gemeinsamer Teiler, ggT)

Definition: Let a,b∈Na, b \in \mathbb{N}a,b∈N. The greatest common divisor of aaa and bbb is the largest natural number ddd such that d∣ad|ad∣a and d∣bd|bd∣b. It is denoted by ggT(a,b)\text{ggT}(a, b)ggT(a,b).
For a,b∈Za, b \in \mathbb{Z}a,b∈Z, we define ggT(a,b):=ggT(∣a∣,∣b∣)\text{ggT}(a, b) := \text{ggT}(|a|, |b|)ggT(a,b):=ggT(∣a∣,∣b∣).

3.3. Euclidean Algorithm (Euklidischer Algorithmus)

The ggT\text{ggT}ggT can be computed using the Euclidean Algorithm.
A sequence of divisions with remainders is performed until the remainder becomes 000.
The last non-zero remainder is the desired ggT\text{ggT}ggT.

Procedure (for a=161,b=119a = 161, b = 119a=161,b=119):

  1. 161=1⋅119+42161 = 1 \cdot 119 + 42161=1⋅119+42
  2. 119=2⋅42+35119 = 2 \cdot 42 + 35119=2⋅42+35
  3. 42=1⋅35+742 = 1 \cdot 35 + 742=1⋅35+7
  4. 35=5⋅7+035 = 5 \cdot 7 + 035=5⋅7+0

The last non-zero remainder is 7, hence ggT(161,119)=7\text{ggT}(161, 119) = 7ggT(161,119)=7.

Theorem (Property of ggT):
If d∣ad|ad∣a and d∣bd|bd∣b, then d∣ggT(a,b)d|\text{ggT}(a, b)d∣ggT(a,b).

3.4. Bézout’s Lemma (Lemma von Bézout)

Theorem (Lemma von Bézout): Let a,b∈Za, b \in \mathbb{Z}a,b∈Z. We can find integers x,yx, yx,y that satisfy the equation
aX+bY=ggT(a,b)aX + bY = \text{ggT}(a, b)aX+bY=ggT(a,b).

Proof Strategy: This result is obtained by running the Euclidean Algorithm “from bottom to top” in reverse.

Example (for 161X+119Y=7161X + 119Y = 7161X+119Y=7):

  1. Start (third-to-last line): 7=42−1⋅357 = 42 - 1 \cdot 357=42−1⋅35
  2. Back-substitution (from line 2): 7=42−1⋅(119−2⋅42)=−1⋅119+3⋅427 = 42 - 1 \cdot (119 - 2 \cdot 42) = -1 \cdot 119 + 3 \cdot 427=42−1⋅(119−2⋅42)=−1⋅119+3⋅42
  3. Back-substitution (from line 1): 7=−1⋅119+3⋅(161−1⋅119)=3⋅161−4⋅1197 = -1 \cdot 119 + 3 \cdot (161 - 1 \cdot 119) = 3 \cdot 161 - 4 \cdot 1197=−1⋅119+3⋅(161−1⋅119)=3⋅161−4⋅119

We can set x:=3x := 3x:=3 and y:=−4y := -4y:=−4.

3.5. Modular Arithmetic (Modulare Arithmetik)

Modular arithmetic is a system of arithmetic for integers where numbers “wrap around” when they reach a certain value, called the modulus.

Definition of Congruence (Definition der Kongruenz)

Let a,b∈Za, b \in \mathbb{Z}a,b∈Z and n∈N+n \in \mathbb{N}^{+}n∈N+.
We say that aaa and bbb are congruent modulo nnn (kongruent modulo nnn), written as a≡b(modn)a \equiv b \pmod{n}a≡b(modn), if aaa and bbb have the same remainder when divided by nnn.

Examples: 27≡7≡2(mod5)27 \equiv 7 \equiv 2 \pmod{5}27≡7≡2(mod5), and 2≡−3(mod5)2 \equiv -3 \pmod{5}2≡−3(mod5).
Every integer aaa is congruent modulo nnn to exactly one of the numbers: 0,1,2,…,n−10, 1, 2, \dots, n-10,1,2,…,n−1.

Equivalence Property (Übung):
A crucial property is that a≡b(modn)a \equiv b \pmod{n}a≡b(modn) if and only if nnn divides the difference (a−b)(a - b)(a−b), i.e., n∣(a−b)n | (a - b)n∣(a−b).

Arithmetic Operations (Arithmetische Operationen)

Theorem (Satz):
Let a,b,c,d∈Za, b, c, d \in \mathbb{Z}a,b,c,d∈Z and let n∈Z∖{0}n \in \mathbb{Z} \setminus \{0\}n∈Z∖{0} (non-zero integer).
If we have a≡b(modn)a \equiv b \pmod{n}a≡b(modn) and c≡d(modn)c \equiv d \pmod{n}c≡d(modn), then the following properties hold:

  1. Addition: a+c≡b+d(modn)a + c \equiv b + d \pmod{n}a+c≡b+d(modn)
  2. Multiplication: ac≡bd(modn)ac \equiv bd \pmod{n}ac≡bd(modn)

Reasoning (Beweis) for Addition:
The premise a≡b(modn)a \equiv b \pmod{n}a≡b(modn) and c≡d(modn)c \equiv d \pmod{n}c≡d(modn) means nnn divides (b−a)(b - a)(b−a) and nnn divides (d−c)(d - c)(d−c).
Since nnn divides both, it must divide their sum: n∣[(b−a)+(d−c)]n | [(b - a) + (d - c)]n∣[(b−a)+(d−c)], which simplifies to n∣[(b+d)−(a+c)]n | [(b + d) - (a + c)]n∣[(b+d)−(a+c)].
By the equivalence property, this means b+d≡a+c(modn)b + d \equiv a + c \pmod{n}b+d≡a+c(modn).

3.6. Applications of Modular Arithmetic

Proof by Case Separation (Fallunterscheidung)

Theorem (Satz):
Let n∈Zn \in \mathbb{Z}n∈Z. Then the square of nnn is congruent to 000 or 111 modulo 3 (n2≡0(mod3)n^2 \equiv 0 \pmod{3}n2≡0(mod3) or n2≡1(mod3)n^2 \equiv 1 \pmod{3}n2≡1(mod3)).

Proof Strategy:
We analyze the three possible cases for nnn modulo 3:

  • Case 1 (n≡0(mod3)n \equiv 0 \pmod{3}n≡0(mod3)): Then n2≡02≡0(mod3)n^2 \equiv 0^2 \equiv 0 \pmod{3}n2≡02≡0(mod3).
  • Case 2 (n≡1(mod3)n \equiv 1 \pmod{3}n≡1(mod3)): Then n2≡12≡1(mod3)n^2 \equiv 1^2 \equiv 1 \pmod{3}n2≡12≡1(mod3).
  • Case 3 (n≡2(mod3)n \equiv 2 \pmod{3}n≡2(mod3)): Then n2≡22≡4≡1(mod3)n^2 \equiv 2^2 \equiv 4 \equiv 1 \pmod{3}n2≡22≡4≡1(mod3).

Since the result holds in all cases, the theorem is proven.

1.1. Propositional Logic (Aussagenlogik)

Propositional logic, or Aussagenlogik, is a fundamental branch of mathematical logic that deals with propositions and the logical relationships between them. It provides a formal system for reasoning and analyzing arguments where the smallest unit is a simple statement that can be definitively labeled as either true or false.

Example: “The sky is blue.” (This is a proposition, as it’s a statement that is true).
Example: “2 + 2 = 5.” (This is also a proposition, though it is false).
Non-Example: “What time is it?” (This is a question, not a statement with a truth value).

We usually point them as big characters such: A, B, C

1.2. Logical Operators (Logische Junktoren)

Logical operators, or connectives, are symbols used to combine simple propositions into more complex, compound propositions. The truth value of the compound proposition depends entirely on the truth values of its parts.

1.3. Building Complex Expressions

Think of simple propositions (p,q) as individual LEGO bricks and the logical connectors (∧,∨,→,¬) as the studs and tubes that connect them. On their own, the bricks are simple, but by combining them, you can construct intricate structures.

These compound expressions allow you to model sophisticated real-world scenarios and arguments. You can chain together multiple operations, using parentheses to define the order, just like in arithmetic.

For example, let’s model the statement: “If it is Tuesday and the sun is shining, then I will not go to work.”

First, define the simple propositions:

   p: It is Tuesday.

   q: The sun is shining.

   r: I will go to work.

Now, combine them using the connectors:

   "It is Tuesday and the sun is shining" becomes: (p∧q)

   "I will not go to work" becomes: ¬r

   "If (p and q), then (not r)" becomes the final compound proposition:

   (p∧q)→¬r

1.4. Forms of Complex Statements (Formen komplexer Aussagen)

Complex statements (or formulas—Formeln) are categorized based on their truth values across all possible assignments of truth values to their atomic propositions.

These four categories define the behavior of the formula:

German TermEnglish TermConditionDescription
TautologieTautologyAlways True (T)A formula that is true regardless of the truth values of its atomic components. It is universally valid.
UnerfüllbarContradictionAlways False (F)A formula that is false regardless of the truth values of its atomic components.
ErfüllbarSatisfiableAt least one True (T)A formula for which there is at least one assignment of truth values that makes the formula true.
WiderlegbarFalsifiableAt least one False (F)A formula for which there is at least one assignment of truth values that makes the formula false.

Key Relationships:

  • A formula is satisfiable if and only if it is not a contradiction (nicht unerfüllbar).
  • A formula is a tautology if and only if its negation is a contradiction.

1.5. Equivalence Chains and Substitution Principle

Truth tables for a formula with nnn distinct atomic propositions (A,B,C,…A, B, C, \dotsA,B,C,…) require 2n2^n2n rows. As the number of variables increases, the table size grows exponentially. For example, a formula with 10 atoms requires 210=10242^{10} = 1024210=1024 rows, making the truth table method practically impossible for complex logical expressions.

We use logical equivalences as algebraic shortcuts to simplify or transform complex formulas, thereby avoiding the exponential workload of a full truth table analysis. Two complex statements (formulas) F1F_1F1​ and F2F_2F2​ are logically equivalent (written as F1↔F2F_1 \leftrightarrow F_2F1​↔F2​) if and only if they have the exact same truth table.

The Substitution Principle (Substitutionsprinzip)

The substitution principle provides the justification for using equivalence chains:

If a formula FFF contains a part (a subformula) SSS, and you know that SSS has the exact same truth table as another formula S′S'S′, you are always safe to replace SSS with S′S'S′ within FFF. The resulting formula F′F'F′ is guaranteed to be logically equivalent to the original FFF.

Simply Put: Equivalent parts can be substituted without affecting the whole.

In Notation: If S↔S′S \leftrightarrow S'S↔S′, and F′F'F′ results from replacing SSS with S′S'S′ in FFF, then F↔F′F \leftrightarrow F'F↔F′.

This principle allows you to use your well-known patterns (like De Morgan’s Laws) as a set of algebraic rules to prove equivalences without building large truth tables.
