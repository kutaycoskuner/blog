Devlog: Color Quantization and Outlines

An update on my pixel scene rendering studies. Recently I focused on color quantization and outline rendering to push the scene toward a more retro aesthetic.

Instead of aiming for realistic lighting, I decided to lean into the look of older games. Many early games used limited color palettes due to hardware constraints, and that limitation eventually became a distinctive and cozy art style.

  1. Color Quantization To simulate this effect, I quantize the lighting by multiplying, dividing, and flooring each pixel’s color value, reducing the number of possible colors.

One issue appeared immediately: when lighting intensity became too low, pixels turned completely black. To prevent this, I introduced a minimum lighting threshold. If the calculated light falls below this value, the pixel color falls back to the ambient lighting contribution instead.

Another challenge appeared on surfaces such as grass patches. Because lighting was calculated per-fragment, adjacent pixels on the same quad could end up with slightly different quantized colors, producing visible artifacts. Moving the lighting calculation to the vertex shader helped distribute the colors more evenly across the surface.


Color quantization of point light

  1. Screen-Space Outlining Outlines are not something that exists in the real world, but they help significantly with object readability and shape definition, especially in stylized rendering.

For a general solution that works across the whole scene, I implemented screen-space outlines using a multi-pass approach. First, the scene generates a depth texture, which represents the distance of each pixel from the camera. By sampling neighboring pixels and comparing depth differences, edges between objects can be detected and highlighted.


Rendering depth texture

However, depth alone is not always enough. When objects get close to the ground or other surfaces, the depth difference becomes very small and edges disappear.

To improve detection, I added another pass that stores linearized screen-space normals. Since normals encode the direction of surfaces, they reveal edges even when depth differences are minimal. By combining depth and normal differences, the outline detection becomes much more robust.


Rendering object normals

Finally, to prevent outlines from appearing over objects that should occlude them, I adjusted rendering layers to control when the outline pass is applied.

On the next step, I’m planning to implement pixelation and add more environmental details to the scene. If you have any questions, I’d be happy to answer them. I’m also open to any suggestions or feedback.
